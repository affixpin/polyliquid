1. Executive Summary
Polyliquid is a Decentralized Delegated Reputation Oracle designed to replace existing oracle systems for prediction markets. The protocol combines delegated staking with a reputation-based voting mechanism to create an oracle that is orders of magnitude more expensive to attack than current solutions.
Core innovation: Voting weight is determined by W = LP_stake × Reputation, where reputation is earned through months of correct voting and cannot be purchased. This creates a system where attacking the oracle requires corrupting voters who have significant economic skin in the game.

2. The Problem: Oracle Vulnerability
Prediction markets require oracles to determine the outcome of events. For subjective events (politics, economics, culture), automated oracles like Chainlink cannot be used. Human-powered oracles are required.
The dominant solution today is UMA (Universal Market Access), which uses a token-weighted voting system called the Data Verification Mechanism (DVM). UMA's fundamental flaw: voting power equals token holdings. This means influence can be purchased instantly.


2.1 UMA Attack Economics
An attacker can acquire enough UMA tokens to control a vote for approximately $750 in transaction costs. The attack on March 2025 demonstrated a $7M market manipulation, and the July 2025 dispute involved $160M at risk.
The core vulnerability: there is no time-based component to trust in UMA. A whale can buy tokens today and control tomorrow's vote.


2.2 Market Size
Total prediction market volume in 2025 reached approximately $44B. Of this, approximately $20B (30%) represents subjective events requiring human oracles. At 0.2% commission, this represents $40M in annual oracle fees.
The market is segmented: sports (~50%, objective, uses data feeds), crypto prices (~20%, uses Chainlink), and politics/economics/culture (~30%, subjective, requires human oracles). Polyliquid targets the third segment.

3. Protocol Design
3.1 Core Mechanism: Delegated Reputation Staking
The protocol operates through three layers:
Layer 1: LP Delegation. Liquidity Providers choose voters (vouters) based on their track record and delegate capital to them. Voters stake this capital on market outcomes. Commission rates are freely negotiated between LP and voter (5–80%).
Layer 2: Commit-Reveal Voting. Voters submit encrypted votes (commit phase), then reveal them. Voting weight is determined by W = LP_stake × Reputation. Consensus requires 50% + 1 point of weighted votes.
Layer 3: Dispute Resolution via DAO. Any trader can dispute a Round 1 result by posting a bond equal to 2% of the market size. The DAO then votes over 48 hours to confirm or overturn the result.


3.2 Reputation Formula
R(t) = Σ LP_stake × 0.995^(days since vote)
Each correct vote adds the LP stake amount to the voter's reputation. Old votes decay at 0.5% per day, giving a half-life of 138 days.
Reputation ceiling = daily_stake / 0.005
For an elite voter staking $1M per day, the maximum achievable reputation is $1M / 0.005 = $200M. This ceiling is reached asymptotically after approximately 12–18 months of consistent voting.
If a voter stops voting, reputation decays to half in 138 days. This protects against abandoned accounts and "sleeping" voters accumulating outsized influence.


3.3 Voting Weight: κ = 1.0 (Sybil-Neutral)
W = LP_stake × Rep^1.0
The exponent κ = 1.0 (linear) is chosen for Sybil-neutrality: splitting an account into two provides no advantage, but also no disadvantage. This eliminates both the incentive to create fake accounts and the centralization risk of super-linear weighting.

4. Slashing Mechanisms
4.1 Reputation Slashing
S_rep = 50% × (W_majority − 50%) / 50%
R_new = R × (1 − S_rep)
Reputation slashing is proportional to the margin of the vote. A close vote (51/49) results in a 1% slash, while a clear consensus (80/20) results in a 30% slash. If a voter loses a DAO dispute, reputation is slashed 100% to zero.

5.2 Cascade of Destruction After Attack
When a voter participates in an attack that is overturned by DAO:
Step 1: DAO overturns R1 result → attacker's reputation is slashed to zero.
Step 2: Reputation = 0 → LP slash risk jumps from 0.5% to 20%.

Step 4: No LP = no stake = no weight = no rewards = no income.
Step 5: Voter restarts from zero as a newcomer. Recovery takes approximately 12 months.
Step 6: Even after 12 months, LP may not return. The market remembers.

8. Attack Resistance Analysis
8.1 Direct Voter Bribery
An attacker must bribe voters whose combined weight exceeds 50%. With κ = 1.0 and 30/30/34 distribution, no single tier can achieve this alone. The minimum viable coalition requires at least 5 elite + 20 top voters (25 people).
Coordination difficulty: each additional conspirator exponentially increases the risk of exposure. One honest participant can expose the entire scheme because all votes are verifiable on-chain after the reveal phase.


8.2 Reputation Farming
Can an attacker build reputation from scratch to attack later? Time required: ~12–18 months of consistent correct voting with $1M daily stake to reach elite status ($200M rep). During this time, the attacker provides genuine economic value to the system.
Cost: $1M in LP capital locked for 12–18 months, plus commission fees paid to protocol. The attacker would earn reputation but also build dependencies (LP relationships, income streams) that make the attack economically irrational.


8.3 Sybil Attack
With κ = 1.0 (linear weight), splitting one account into multiple provides zero advantage. One account with $200M reputation has exactly the same weight as two accounts with $100M each. This makes Sybil attacks economically neutral — no incentive to create fake accounts.


8.4 DAO Corruption
Could DAO Polyliquid voters be bribed to vote incorrectly?
DAO Polyliquid receives 25% of slashes when it overturns R1. This creates a theoretical conflict of interest: DAO could falsely overturn correct R1 results to earn slashes.


Why this is economically irrational:
The 25% slash reward for any single dispute is small relative to the Polyliquid token market cap. A false DAO vote would immediately damage protocol credibility, causing token price collapse that dwarfs any slash income.
Example: if Polyliquid has a $500M market cap and a dispute produces $3M in slashes, DAO would earn $750K (25% of $3M). But a credibility-damaging false vote could easily cause a 10%+ token price drop = $50M+ loss. The ratio is 67:1 against corruption.
Additionally: all DAO votes are on-chain and publicly auditable; voters who participated in the attack lose their reputation permanently; clients (Polymarket etc.) would immediately terminate their relationship; the entire voter community would exit.


8.5 Client-Side Corruption (Polymarket DAO)
What if the client's DAO (e.g., Polymarket) tries to manipulate outcomes?
The client does not control Polyliquid's DAO. The dispute resolution is handled by Polyliquid token holders, not the client. The client receives 25% of slashes and 100% of spam bonds, but cannot influence the vote.
For the client to benefit from manipulation, they would need to collude with Polyliquid DAO voters. But with a $20B+ market cap (Polymarket) or $500M+ (Polyliquid), the risk-reward ratio makes this irrational.


8.6 Bond Manipulation on Large Markets
For a $10B market, the dispute bond = 2% = $200M. If the dispute is spam, the client receives 100% of the bond ($200M). Could this motivate false spam classifications?
No — the DAO Polyliquid (not the client) decides whether a dispute is spam or legitimate. The client has no vote in this decision. And for DAO Polyliquid, confirming a legitimate dispute as spam would mean endorsing an attack, destroying protocol credibility for zero direct benefit (DAO PL gets $0 from spam bond).
